# Reference — LLM / generative-engine crawler directory

A working reference of the crawlers that matter for GEO. Two categories behave very differently and should be decided separately, not lumped together:

- **Training crawlers** — collect content to train future model versions. Allowing one does not get you cited today; it may (or may not) influence a future model's latent knowledge.
- **Retrieval / live-fetch crawlers** — power search indexing or on-demand fetches during a live user query. Blocking one of these directly removes your chance of being cited in that engine's answers today.

Robots.txt is advisory, not an enforcement wall — compliant bots honor it, non-compliant ones don't always. Verified crawlers and directive behavior below per [Priso's AI-bot robots.txt reference](https://priso.nl/en/blog/ai-bots-robots-txt-allow-or-block); consult each vendor's own docs for updates, since bot rosters change.

## Working principles

1. **Separate training from retrieval before deciding.** A single "block all AI bots" line conflates a training-data decision with a today's-citations decision — they have different costs and benefits.
2. **Explicit beats wildcard.** Most AI crawlers only reliably honor their own named `User-agent` block; don't assume `User-agent: *` covers them.
3. **Robots.txt is a policy signal, not an enforcement wall.** For crawlers with a track record of non-compliance, back the robots.txt entry with server-level controls (Cloudflare WAF AI-scraper rules, platform firewall/user-agent rules, rate limiting).
4. **Recheck the roster.** New crawlers and renamed user-agents appear regularly — treat this table as a snapshot to verify against current vendor documentation before each robots.txt audit (see `examples/robots-config-trace.md` and Recipe B in `HOWTO.md`).
