# On-page checklist — per new post

Run this checklist before publishing any new post, and re-run the relevant sections when refreshing an existing one (`HOWTO.md` recipe c).

## Title tag

- [ ] ≤60 characters (measure, don't estimate — truncation cuts off mid-word in the SERP).
- [ ] Leads with the primary keyword or the phrasing closest to how the target query is actually typed.
- [ ] Distinct from every other title already live on the domain — no two pages competing for the same exact promise.
- [ ] One clear promise, not two or three keywords stitched together.

**Formulas:**
- Direct-match: `{Primary Keyword}: {Specific Angle}` — e.g., "Email Segmentation: A Step-by-Step Guide"
- Benefit-led: `How to {Outcome} {Without/With Constraint}` — e.g., "How to Cut Bounce Rate Without Losing List Size"
- Specificity-led: `{Number} {Thing} for {Audience} ({Year})` — e.g., "7 Content Pruning Mistakes to Avoid in 2026"
- Comparison: `{A} vs {B}: Which {Outcome} in {Year}` — e.g., "Consolidate vs Redirect: Which Fixes Cannibalization Faster"

## Meta description

- [ ] 140-155 characters.
- [ ] States the specific value/payoff of the page, not a restatement of the title.
- [ ] Includes the primary keyword naturally, not forced.
- [ ] Ends on a complete thought / reason to click — never cut off mid-sentence.
- [ ] Avoids generic phrasing ("everything you need to know," "the ultimate guide") unless the page can actually prove it's the most complete one live.

**Pattern:** `{What the page delivers, specific}. {Proof point or differentiator}. {Optional: freshness marker}.`
Example: "We tested 9 project management tools for 3 weeks each. Compare pricing, AI features, and team-size fit — updated for 2026."

## H1 and headings

- [ ] Exactly one H1 per page.
- [ ] H1 matches the reader's intent; not an identical copy-paste of the title tag.
- [ ] Every H2 maps to a distinct subtopic — no two H2s competing for the same idea.
- [ ] No heading level skipped (H2 to H4 with no H3 in between).
- [ ] Headings read as a coherent outline on their own, with no body text — if a stranger read just the headings, could they summarize the page?

**H2 formulas:**
- Question-form for FAQ-style subtopics: "How much does {X} cost?"
- Direct subtopic naming for guide-style subtopics: "Pricing," "Setup," "Common mistakes"
- Comparison-table intro: "{A} vs {B}: {Dimension} Compared"

## URL

- [ ] Short — fewer words is better; drop stop words (the, a, of) unless needed for meaning.
- [ ] All lowercase, hyphen-separated, no underscores or special characters.
- [ ] Keyword-relevant, matching the primary target phrase.
- [ ] Locked before publishing — changing it after indexing/backlinks requires a 301 redirect and risks a temporary ranking dip.
- [ ] No date stamps in the URL unless the content is genuinely date-specific (dated URLs make evergreen refreshes look stale even after updating).

## Image alt text

- [ ] Every image has alt text — no image ships with an empty or placeholder `alt=""` unless it's purely decorative.
- [ ] Alt text describes what the image actually shows, not the target keyword stuffed in.
- [ ] Alt text is concise — one clear sentence or phrase, not a paragraph.
- [ ] Filenames are descriptive before upload (`email-segmentation-flowchart.png`, not `IMG_4821.png`) — filename is a minor but real signal.
- [ ] Screenshots and original graphics are preferred over stock photos where the topic supports a first-hand experience marker (E-E-A-T).

**Pattern:** `{What's shown}, {relevant context if not obvious from surrounding text}`
Example: `alt="Screenshot of the email segmentation dashboard showing three engagement tiers"`

## Internal linking rules

- [ ] At least one hub-spoke link (up to the hub, or down to a sibling spoke) per new page.
- [ ] At least one contextual in-body link with descriptive anchor text (never "click here," "read more," "this article").
- [ ] At least one other live page links back in before or immediately after publishing — no page ships as an orphan.
- [ ] Breadcrumb trail present and matches the site's actual hierarchy (Home > Category > Page).
- [ ] Anchor text names the destination topic, not a generic action phrase.

## E-E-A-T minimums

- [ ] Author byline present, linking to a real bio with credentials relevant to the topic.
- [ ] At least one first-hand experience marker where the topic supports it (a test result, an original screenshot, a specific named example — "we tried," "in our test," not generic restated knowledge).
- [ ] Every external statistic, quote, or "studies show" claim links to its primary source — verified by actually opening the source, never cited blind.
- [ ] Visible publish date; visible "last updated" date if the page has been refreshed.
- [ ] For YMYL topics (health, finance, legal, safety): credentials are specific and checkable, not just a job title.

## Semantic coverage (run after the above, per SKILL.md)

- [ ] Every sub-question a genuine expert would expect the topic to answer is addressed somewhere on the page.
- [ ] Related entities (tools, standards, people, concepts an expert would mention) are named, not omitted.
- [ ] Vocabulary matches how top-ranking competitor pages and "people also ask" boxes phrase the topic — not artificially varied keyword synonyms stuffed for density.
- [ ] A reader's likely follow-up question is answered on-page, not left for them to search again.
